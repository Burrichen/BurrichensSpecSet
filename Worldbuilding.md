You already have strong regional identities. Each location has a recognisable atmosphere, a distinctive form of Axius, and an obvious genre promise. The next stage is not adding more inspirations. It is turning these concepts into functioning societies, connected political regions, and playable adventure environments.

The main danger is that each place remains “Japan with Pokémon,” “Norway with magic snow,” or “Dune with diesel.” The inspirations should become foundations rather than descriptions. Players should eventually recognise Shinmira, Hjarthland and Halingo as themselves.

1. Establish the central design question of each region

Give every region a thematic conflict that influences its politics, characters, settlements and adventures.

Region	Central thematic question
Ordon	Who benefits from progress, and who gets abandoned by it?
Shinmira	How much of yourself can change before you cease to be yourself?
Hjarthland	Can something be both sacred and dangerously exploitable?
Halingo	What happens when abundance is controlled by people who manufacture scarcity?
Iron Coast	How much humanity must be surrendered merely to survive?
Herovir	Is corruption something to resist, exploit or adapt to?
Armada	Does freedom from nations actually prevent hierarchy and oppression?

These questions should appear repeatedly. They should affect wealthy and poor people differently, produce disagreements between reasonable characters, and create decisions without obvious correct answers.

2. Define the universal rules of Axius

Axius is your setting’s strongest unifying idea. It should feel like one phenomenon with different regional expressions rather than seven unrelated magic systems.

Create an Axius rules document answering:

Why does Axius take different forms?
Is its form determined by geology, belief, climate, history or something beneath reality?
Can one regional form be transported elsewhere?
Does transported Axius remain stable?
What happens when two forms interact?
Can Axius be created, depleted or destroyed?
What is the long-term cost of using it?
Who understands it best?
What does the average person believe about it?
What is everyone dangerously wrong about?

For each regional form, define the same properties:

Property	Example
Physical form	Ore, crystal, atmosphere, ichor, rainwater
Primary benefit	Technology, transformation, magic, fuel
Immediate danger	Explosion, death, mutation, poisoning
Long-term consequence	Dependence, environmental change, inherited corruption
Controller	Government, church, companies, clans, nobody
Social taboo	Who is forbidden from using it?
Black-market use	What do criminals do with it?
Interaction	What happens when exposed to another regional form?

The interaction between Hjarthland’s atmosphere and Ordon’s ore is especially important. It establishes that Axius combinations may be more powerful than either substance alone. That immediately creates an international arms race.

3. Connect the regions economically and politically

At present, each region is compelling individually. Now give them reasons to affect one another.

A strong initial world network might be:

Ordon wants Hjarthland’s atmospheric Axius, Shinmiran crystals and Iron Coast artifacts.
Hjarthland fears another Ordon invasion but depends upon the silent engine.
Shinmira faces foreign interest in crystals, transformed creatures and empowered humans.
Halingo exports Ichor while its rulers deliberately keep peripheral communities dependent.
Iron Coast needs imported food, metal and medicine but possesses priceless otherworldly salvage.
Herovir is officially isolated, although every major power secretly wants samples of its radioactive ore.
Armada moves contraband, refugees, spies and technology between places that cannot trade openly.

Then decide:

What does each region desperately need?
What does it possess that others want?
Who is its traditional enemy?
Who is its uncomfortable ally?
What international incident could begin a war tomorrow?

This will make travel meaningful. Entering a new country should change what the characters’ equipment, citizenship, reputation and knowledge mean.

4. Flesh out each region through specific design problems
Ordon

Ordon already has the strongest position in the world: it is the technological and political centre. Its next requirement is internal variation.

Design the country as concentric stages of industrial development:

The capital is dazzling, overcrowded and almost impossibly advanced.
Industrial satellite cities manufacture its technology.
Market towns sit between steam-powered modernity and older traditions.
Rural regions resent being taxed, mined or ignored by the capital.
Remote communities may regard Ordon’s machines as foreign intrusions despite technically belonging to the same nation.

Develop:

Three to five capital districts with different classes and functions.
The institution controlling the Axius ore.
The relationship between government, industrialists, inventors and workers.
A rural movement resisting technological expansion.
The terrible cost hidden beneath Ordon’s prosperity.
What the Eye of Gold and Morgwen mean to modern Ordon, rather than only historically.

Campaign promise: political intrigue, industrial mysteries, organised crime, class conflict and journeys from dazzling technology into neglected old fantasy.

Shinmira

The major design task is determining how crystal transformation has reshaped society.

You need rules for:

Whether transformed animals remain ordinary animals mentally.
Whether transformation is reversible.
Whether creatures are companions, citizens, weapons, livestock or sacred beings.
How surviving humans with abilities are treated.
Whether empowered people are registered, recruited, worshipped or imprisoned.
How intentional crystal exposure differs from accidents.
Whether powers are hereditary.
Who owns naturally occurring crystals.

Create original setting terminology for the creatures and human manifestations. Pokémon and Stands are useful design shorthand, but the final versions should have their own metaphysics, aesthetics and cultural meaning.

The “modern and old Japan” influence can become an internal conflict: technologically connected cities may regulate crystals scientifically, while older communities treat transformation through religious traditions and ancestral obligations.

Campaign promise: creature expeditions, rival schools, transformation mysteries, tournaments, spirit politics and questions of personhood.

Hjarthland

Hjarthland needs to become somewhere people can actually inhabit rather than merely an impressive frozen environment.

Determine:

How food is grown or acquired.
How settlements are heated.
How people travel during permanent winter.
How atmospheric Axius affects birth, illness, animals and architecture.
Whether everyone can use magic or only particular individuals.
Why magic is frightening despite being worshipped.
What the ouroboros in the sky physically does.
Whether it is a creature, atmospheric phenomenon, god or containment mechanism.

The silent Ordon engine should be a major setting mystery. Decide what it powers, why it was permitted to remain, who maintains it, and what would happen if it stopped. It could heat a city, hold back the ice, suppress something underground or quietly extract Axius for Ordon.

The old war should have visible scars: abandoned machines, mixed-border families, forbidden ruins, veterans, propaganda and unexploded Axius weapons.

Campaign promise: mythic horror, sacred wilderness, survival, forbidden expeditions and an approaching second resource war.

Halingo

Halingo’s next task is replacing “general wasteland oppression” with specific systems of power.

First decide how the Diesel Barons relate to your previous Sorcerer-King concept. They might be the same rulers under a different title, competing systems, or industrial dynasties serving ancient kings. Resolve this before building further.

Each major ruler should control something different:

Ichor extraction
Rail transport
Water
Weapons manufacturing
Food production
Banking and debt
Communication or information

This makes their territories mechanically and culturally distinct.

You also need to answer why impoverished settlements cannot simply revolt or leave. Perhaps company debt follows them, trains require papers, water sources are guarded, or Ichor exposure makes certain medicine essential.

Develop the rail system carefully. In a wasteland, railways are not just scenery; they determine where cities exist, how armies move and which communities survive.

Campaign promise: train robberies, company wars, desert survival, revolutionary politics, gunslingers and expeditions into Ichor-changed wastelands.

Iron Coast

The Iron Coast has an excellent gameplay loop already:

Something washes ashore → someone needs it recovered → another faction wants it → using it has a terrible consequence.

Now formalise what the sea produces. Create broad categories:

Useful otherworldly technology
Living organisms
Magical remains
Contaminated cargo
Impossible structures
Messages or evidence from elsewhere
Objects that appear useful but are actively dangerous

Steelbacks need to become a culture rather than only a profession. Establish how steel is attached, what it costs, whether it can be removed, how Steelbacks are trained, and how ordinary people regard them. Their package routes can form the veins of the region.

Define the acidic rain precisely. It should create predictable pressures without becoming so destructive that settlement is impossible.

Also decide whether Iron Coast is the formal name for the region previously called Skgurr, or whether Skgurr is a country, culture or major settlement within it.

Campaign promise: courier journeys, coastal horror, salvage expeditions, body modification, isolated communities and unknowable objects from beyond the sea.

Herovir

Herovir is currently the least developed because “radioactive evil gothic place” describes its mood but not its society.

The first question is:

Why does anyone still live there?

Possible answers include ancestral obligation, rare resources, inability to leave, adaptation, religious belief, exile, or the fact that some inhabitants no longer consider the corruption harmful.

Give Herovir a distinctive internal conflict. For example:

Unchanged humans want to purge corruption.
Adapted communities believe mutation is necessary for survival.
Aristocrats use controlled exposure to preserve themselves.
Religious orders interpret radiation sickness as divine revelation.
Foreign researchers treat inhabitants as specimens.

Create contamination gradients rather than making the entire region equally hostile. There should be relatively safe settlements, restricted zones, ruined corridors and places where reality has almost completely failed.

Its evil should also have a cause and worldview. “Evil things live here” becomes more effective when those things have built societies, rituals and justifications.

You may also want to settle whether Herovir replaces or contains the earlier Bratislor concept.

Campaign promise: gothic tragedy, mutation, ruined technology, morally compromised survival and expeditions into places that rewrite living bodies.

Armada

Armada needs practical infrastructure more than additional atmosphere.

Replace “ships taped together” in the final presentation with a more convincing construction process: ships chained, bridged, riveted and built over until their original hulls become districts beneath newer layers.

Determine:

How Armada moves.
How it obtains clean water.
How it produces food.
How damaged ships are replaced.
Whether ships voluntarily join.
Who governs it.
How citizenship works.
What happens to people who break its laws.
Who controls navigation.
Why nations tolerate it.
What prevents a single fire or storm from destroying everything.

Its districts can be based on the original vessels: an old warship becomes the military quarter, a pleasure barge becomes the wealthy district, cargo vessels become markets, and half-flooded lower decks become slums or criminal territory.

Armada should not be uniformly anarchic. It will be more interesting if its mythology celebrates freedom while entrenched captains, navigators and water-keepers possess immense power.

Campaign promise: maritime intrigue, vertical exploration, piracy, ship politics, smuggling and conflict over where the entire nation travels next.

5. Give every region ordinary life

This is the fantasy-author step that setting documents frequently overlook.

For each region, describe:

What an ordinary family eats.
What work occupies most people.
What children are taught.
How people marry, mourn and celebrate.
What is considered rude.
What people fear after dark.
What outsiders misunderstand.
What residents complain about.
What cheap entertainment exists.
What a poor home and wealthy home look like.

This prevents cultures from being defined only by rulers, monsters and aesthetics.

Also create contradictions. Hjarthlanders can worship magic while distrusting spellcasters. Ordon citizens can genuinely love technological progress while benefiting from exploitation. Halingo workers can admire the trains that enforce their dependence. Armada can value freedom while denying it to indebted crew.

Contradiction produces believable cultures.

6. Build locations at three scales

For each region, create three layers.

Regional scale

Define borders, trade routes, climate, major powers and three important historical events.

Settlement scale

Create:

One capital or major city
One ordinary town
One isolated or unusual settlement
One ruined settlement
One border community

The ordinary town is particularly important. It shows what everyday life looks like when nothing spectacular is occurring.

Adventure-site scale

Create three places designed specifically for exploration:

One inhabited location with social conflict
One dangerous wilderness location
One dungeon, ruin or restricted industrial site

Do not write every village before doing this. Five strong settlements and three adventure sites will provide more usable material than fifty names.

7. Prepare each region for actual play

For a professional DM, every regional document should eventually contain:

Three active factions
Six memorable NPCs
Three current crises
Ten rumours
Three regional encounters
Three adventure sites
One dangerous journey
One local festival or public event
One escalating regional clock
One decision that permanently changes the region

A regional clock is especially useful. For example:

Ordon secretly resumes experiments with Hjarthland Axius.
The Hjarthland engine begins slowing.
A massive unidentified object approaches the Iron Coast.
One Diesel Baron gains control of several rival rail lines.
Herovir’s contamination begins appearing outside its borders.
Armada changes course towards a politically forbidden sea.

The world should progress even when the players are elsewhere.

8. Choose one campaign region before detailing everything

Because you prefer a town-centred campaign structure, the best practical next move is to choose one region and create the players’ home settlement there.

That settlement needs:

A reason the characters care about it.
Several people who depend upon them.
Problems too large to resolve immediately.
Buildings or institutions that can improve.
Connections to at least two other regions.
A nearby wilderness and adventure site.
Consequences whenever the party leaves and returns.

The other regions initially need only enough detail to support travellers, rumours, trade and foreign NPCs. Develop them deeply when the campaign begins moving towards them.

Recommended order of work

Your most efficient sequence is:

Standardise names and canon, particularly Hjarthland’s spelling, Iron Coast/Skgurr and Herovir/Bratislor.
Write the universal Axius rules.
Create the political and trade relationships between all seven regions.
Write a one-page regional brief for each location.
Choose the campaign’s starting region.
Build one highly detailed home settlement.
Create three factions and one escalating threat around it.
Design the first three adventures.
Develop neighbouring regions only as they become relevant.
Maintain a timeline recording how player actions alter the world.

Your concepts are already imaginative enough. The setting will now improve most by adding causality: how people survive, who controls resources, what each society believes, what each society hides, and what happens when the different forms of Axius collide.
